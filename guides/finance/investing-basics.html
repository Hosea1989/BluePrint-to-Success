Investing Basics

Introduction

Investing is a powerful tool for building wealth and achieving financial goals. This guide covers the fundamentals of investing and how to get started.

Investment Vehicles

  • Stocks: Ownership shares in a company. Potential for high returns but higher risk.
  • Bonds: Loans to a company or government that pay interest. Generally lower risk than stocks.
  • Mutual Funds: Pooled investments managed by professionals. Instant diversification.
  • ETFs (Exchange-Traded Funds): Similar to mutual funds but trade like stocks. Often lower fees.
  • Real Estate: Property investments for rental income or appreciation.
  • Index Funds: Track a market index like the S&P 500. Low fees and broad diversification.

Risk and Return

  • Understanding Risk: The potential for loss in an investment. All investments carry some risk.
  • Balancing Risk and Return: Higher potential returns generally come with higher risk.
  • Risk Tolerance: Your personal comfort level with potential losses. Depends on age, goals, and financial situation.

Diversification

  • Purpose: Reduces risk by spreading investments across different asset classes.
  • How to Diversify: Invest in a mix of stocks, bonds, and other assets across different sectors and geographies.
  • Don't Put All Eggs in One Basket: Even great companies can fail. Spread your investments.

Getting Started

  1. Pay off high-interest debt first - The guaranteed return beats most investments
  2. Build an emergency fund - 3-6 months of expenses before investing
  3. Take advantage of employer 401(k) match - It's free money
  4. Open a brokerage account - Fidelity, Schwab, Vanguard are popular options
  5. Start with index funds - Low cost, diversified, beginner-friendly
  6. Invest regularly - Dollar-cost averaging reduces timing risk

Account Types

  • 401(k): Employer-sponsored retirement account. Often includes matching.
  • IRA (Traditional): Tax-deductible contributions, taxed on withdrawal.
  • Roth IRA: After-tax contributions, tax-free withdrawals in retirement.
  • Taxable Brokerage: No tax advantages but no restrictions on withdrawals.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Trying to time the market
  • Panic selling during downturns
  • Not diversifying enough
  • Paying high fees
  • Investing money you'll need soon
